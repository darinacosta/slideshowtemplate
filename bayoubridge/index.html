The Fisherman


Shane Doucet

Shane Doucet comes from a long line of Cajun crawfisherman. He has been fishing, exploring and living off of the Atchafalaya Basin since he was a kid. Because of the many pipelines and their accompanying spoil banks cutting through the Basin and creating "Dead Zones," it has become increasingly difficult for Shane and hundreds of other Crawfisherman to make a living.

The Pastor

Pastor Harry Joseph

Harry Joseph is the lead pastor of the 113 year old Mt. Triumph Baptist Church. His community, which is already surrounded by chemical plants and crude oil storage tanks, would be the terminus point for the Bayou Bridge Pipeline. He is a plaintiff in a lawsuit filed by Tulane Environmental Law Clinic to prevent the pipeline.

The Archivist

Jessi Parfait

Jessi Parfait is a member of the United Houma Nation Native American Tribe. She has been the archivist for the tribe for the past 2 years, digitizing and organizing all of their historical information. As a current graduate student at LSU she hopes to use her education to help the tribe preserve their culture and tell their own stories.

The General

Lieutenant General Russel Honore

Russel Honore is a retired 3-star Lieutenant General who served as the 33rd commanding general of the US Army. He was the commander of the Joint Task Force Katrina responsible for coordinating military relief efforts for Hurricane Katrina-affected areas across the Gulf Coast. Bayou Bridge Pipeline Facts

The 162 mile pipeline will travel through approximately 700 bodies of water in Louisiana, including Bayou Lafourche, the drinking water of more than 300,000 people.

According to the Pipeline and Hazardous Materials Safety Administration, the company proposing the pipeline, Energy Transfer Partners, has spilled hazardous liquids near water crossings more than twice as frequently as any other pipeline company in the country this decade.

There has been no third-party Environmental Impact Study (EIS) done on the potential dangers of the Bayou Bridge Pipeline to Louisiana’s water. A coalition of environmental groups and concerned Louisiana citizens are calling on the Governor to order the Corps of Engineers to conduct an EIS before this project is allowed.

The Archivist

The United Houma Nation, a Native American tribe made up of more than 17,000 members, has been living off the land in Louisiana for hundreds of years. Forced migrations have pushed them from North Louisiana into swamplands deemed "uninhabitable" by French and American settlers. The Houma people thrived off of the land and waters for generations, until oil and gas exploration began. Spills, leaks, and landfills have forced them off of their land time and again. Now, the Bayou Bridge Pipeline threatens their drinking water.

THE PASTOR

Fifty miles up the Mississippi river from New Orleans, in the heart of "Cancer Alley," sits the fifth district of St. James. Made up of a collection of small communities, including Freetown, founded by former slaves and free people of color who bought land that had once been part of the Landry-Pedesclaux Plantation, is currently surrounded crude oil storage tanks and chemical plants, and stands to be the terminus point for the proposed Bayou Bridge Pipeline.

The General

Whether it be the rapidly disappearing coastal land, the state’s petrochemical mega-projects, or the region’s extensive pipeline system, Louisiana faces immense environmental challenges. After a long career in service, US Army, Lt. General Russel Honore calls out the state’s vested interest in oil and gas, demanding accountability. The Bayou Bridge pipeline is yet another long term investment in an under-regulated fossil fuel industry that is permanently damaging the state’s air, water and land.

The Fisherman

The Atchafalaya Basin, the largest swamp in North America, has a spiderweb of pipelines that have been cut into its wetlands. Miles of east-west pipelines and their accompanying spoil banks have disrupted the north-south water flow, creating vast areas of stagnant water where crawfish and other wildlife can no longer survive. The Bayou Bridge Pipeline would be another pipeline carved into the Basin, an area that is already enduring irreparable harm from the existing pipelines.
