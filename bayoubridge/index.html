The Fisherman

Shane Doucet

Shane Doucet comes from a long line of Cajun crawfisherman. He has been fishing, exploring and living off of the Atchafalaya Basin since he was a kid. Because of the many pipelines and their accompanying spoil banks cutting through the Basin and creating "Dead Zones," it has become increasingly difficult for Shane and hundreds of other Crawfisherman to make a living.

The Pastor

Pastor Harry Joseph

Harry Joseph is the lead pastor of the 113 year old Mt. Triumph Baptist Church. His community, which is already surrounded by chemical plants and crude oil storage tanks, would be the terminus point for the Bayou Bridge Pipeline. He is a plaintiff in a lawsuit filed by Tulane Environmental Law Clinic to prevent the pipeline.

The Archivist

Archivist

Jessi Parfait is a member of the United Houma Nation Native American Tribe. Jessi has been the archivist for the tribe for the past 2 years, digitizing and organizing all of their historical information. As a current graduate student at LSU she hopes to use her education to help the tribe preserve their culture and tell their own stories.

The General

Lieutenant General Russel Honore

Russel Honore is a retired 3-star Lieutenant General who served as the 33rd commanding general of the US Army. He was the commander of the Joint Task Force Katrina responsible for coordinating military relief efforts for Hurricane Katrina-affected areas across the Gulf Coast. He is the founder of the environmental collective The Green Army.

The Archivist

The United Houma Nation, a Native American tribe made up of more than 17,000 members, has been living off the land in Louisiana for hundreds of years. Forced migrations have pushed them from North Louisiana into swamplands deemed "uninhabitable" by French and American settlers. The Houma people thrived off of the land and waters for generations, until oil and gas exploration began. Spills, leaks, and landfills have forced them off of their land time and again. Now, the Bayou Bridge Pipeline threatens their drinking water.



THE PASTOR

Fifty miles up the Mississippi river from New Orleans, in the heart of "Cancer Alley," sits the fifth district of St. James. A collection of small communities that includes the historic Freetown, a settlement founded by former slaves and free people of color who bought land that had once been part of the Landry-Pedesclaux Plantation. This community would be the terminus point for the planned Bayou Bridge Pipeline.


The General

The Atchafalaya Basin, the largest swamp in North America, has a spiderweb of pipelines across its wetlands. The east to west pipelines and their accompanying spoil banks have disrupted the north to south water flow, thereby creating large areas of stagnant water where crawfish and other wildlife can no longer survive. The Bayou Bridge Pipeline would be another east to west pipeline cutting across the Basin, an area already being irreparably harmed by pipelines and the oil and gas industry.

The Bayou Bridge Pipeline will cross through the drinking water of more than 300,000 people

There has been no third party environmental study
